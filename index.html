01 · The framework
Three swim lanes. One program.
Most technology programs fail because they conflate infrastructure with outcome. The three-swim-lane model separates what must be true (technical infrastructure), what must be built (the activation layer), and what the business actually cares about (outcomes) — and treats each with the discipline it deserves.

02 · Artifact re-imagination
What organizations have vs. what agents need
Every artifact in a real estate organization was built for a reviewer, an auditor, or a regulator. None were built for a machine that must act on them. The shift from audit-era to agent-era requires re-meaning every artifact — not just reformatting it.
Data readiness — audit-ready ≠ agent-ready
The most fundamental shift in the entire program
Today Must become
Audit-ready data — traceable, complete, defensible to a regulator → Agent-ready data — structured for inference, grounded, decision-scoped
Data lineage — where did this number come from? → Decision lineage — why did the system recommend this action?
Definition of data — what a field is called and how it’s typed → Meaning of data — what a value implies in context, for whom
Taxonomies & data dictionaries
Hierarchical labels → nodes and edges

A taxonomy feels structured — it has hierarchy, categories, controlled vocabulary. But hierarchy is a tree, and trees are one-dimensional relationships. What an agent needs is a graph: this asset class relates to this risk profile relates to this approval threshold relates to this reporting obligation.

The moment you add a second dimension of relationship, a taxonomy becomes an ontology, and the work required multiplies by an order of magnitude. Organizations that try to feed a flat taxonomy to an agent will get agents that confidently traverse the wrong paths.

  • Today: Classification trees — static, categorical, maintained in Excel or a data dictionary
  • Must become: Traversable graph with weighted relationships — machine-readable, queryable, version-controlled
Ontologies
Entity model → people-in-the-loop context diagram

Most enterprise ontologies are built by data architects working from system schemas. They capture what entities exist and how they relate technically. They do not capture who touches each entity, in what context, under what authority, and for what purpose.

An agent-ready ontology must include the human layer: the asset manager who reviews this entity every Monday, the approval threshold above which the CFO is required, the context that makes a given data value meaningful vs. noise.

  • Today: Assets, funds, entities — typed and related in a schema
  • Must become: People-in-the-loop context diagrams — who touches each entity, when, why, and with what authority
Policies & procedures
Documented rules → decision hierarchies with lineage

A policy document is written in natural language, optimized for human interpretation, deliberately ambiguous at the edges to give practitioners discretion. An agent cannot exercise discretion — it needs a decision tree with explicit branch conditions, thresholds, exception paths, and escalation triggers.

Rewriting a policy as a decision hierarchy forces the organization to answer questions it has never had to make explicit: what does “material” mean exactly? What is the threshold at which CFO approval is required? The artifact transformation reveals governance gaps that were previously hidden in discretion.

  • Today: Written for human compliance — narrative, interpretable, auditable after the fact
  • Must become: Machine-executable decision hierarchies — every branch has a condition, every escalation has a trigger, every outcome is auditable
Contracts & agreements
Document of record → active obligation graph

A lease is not just a document — it is a set of time-triggered obligations, option windows, co-tenancy clauses, and renewal rights that currently live in a PDF and surface only when someone remembers to look, or when they don’t and something lapses.

An active obligation graph means every clause that has a downstream consequence is a live node that can fire. This is not a data problem. It’s an epistemology problem — you have to decide what the contract means operationally before you can encode it.

  • Today: Stored, searched when a dispute arises, reviewed at renewal
  • Must become: Clauses as live rules triggering downstream actions — expiry alerts, approval workflows, reporting obligations
Reporting & KPIs
Static scorecard → early warning signal system
  • Reports today: Snapshot in time, consumed and filed. Produced for a reviewer.
  • Reports must become: Living signals that trigger action and route to the right decision maker at the right moment
  • KPIs today: Measurement of what happened last period — lagging, descriptive, backward-looking
  • KPIs must become: Leading indicators that prompt before failure — forward-looking, actionable, personalized by role
  • Org charts today: Reporting lines and accountability on paper
  • Org charts must become: Decision authority maps — who can approve what, at which threshold, in which context
Workflows & process maps
Steps for humans → agent orchestration specs

Process maps documented for human execution cannot be handed to an agent. They are missing the four things an agent requires: explicit triggers that fire the workflow, permission boundaries at each step, escalation paths when conditions are not met, and decision lineage at every branch point.

  • Today: Steps documented for human execution — narrative, assumed context, discretion at every edge case
  • Must become: Agent orchestration specs — triggers, permissions, escalation paths, and lineage encoded explicitly
03 · Resourcing model
New roles, not the old org chart with new titles
The instinct is to solve a new problem with the old org chart. That’s where these programs die. The four archetypes below are not job titles you can post on LinkedIn and fill — they are skill clusters that currently exist partially, in scattered people who built them through unusual career paths.
What no longer works
Subject matter expert
Knows the domain, can’t build
Business analyst
Translates, can’t decide
Solutions architect
Designs, can’t operationalize
Project manager
Tracks, doesn’t shape
Vendor impl. consultant
Configures their product, leaves
What the program actually requires
Outcome engineer
Domain fluency + product thinking
Owns the question: what must this system actually do? Sits at the intersection of asset management knowledge and product design instinct. Probably a former operator who taught themselves to build, or a PM who spent years embedded in real estate.
  • Translates business outcomes into system behaviors
  • Defines what “correct” looks like for each decision type
  • Sets the bar for signal vs. noise
  • Validates prototypes with domain operators
Knowledge architect
Ontology + graph + meaning
Owns: how is reality encoded? Exists most frequently in biotech and defense — industries that solved this problem earlier. Rare in real estate. Builds the ontology, forces the governance conversations the organization has never had.
  • Constructs the entity graph and relationship model
  • Forces explicit answers to implicit governance questions
  • Ensures the ontology includes the human context layer
  • Partners with legacy IT to preserve institutional knowledge
Forward-deployed engineer
Full-stack + AI/agent fluency
The rarest archetype. Can ship production code and sit across from a CFO and earn their trust in the same week. Owns: build it where the work is. Works embedded in operations, not isolated in engineering.
  • Produces working prototypes in days, not months
  • Builds against real data from day one
  • Wires agents into actual workflows, not demos
  • Leaves artifacts that the organization can maintain
Trust & operations lead
Risk + audit + change management
Owns: does the org actually adopt? Easiest to find because it maps to existing change management and internal audit profiles — but must be genuinely interested in AI systems, not just governance theater.
  • Maps stakeholder resistance before it calcifies
  • Designs the trust evidence that makes pilots unfalsifiable
  • Translates agent behavior into language auditors accept
  • Builds the COE that survives the program team
What domain experts do in the new model
Domain experts stay essential — but as validators, not drivers. Their job shifts from defining requirements to stress-testing prototypes. They review working software, not Word documents. And critically: their time must be structurally committed, not “we’ll find time.” The outcome engineers and forward-deployed engineers are useless without two hours of protected access to the head of asset management every week.
04 · Program structure
Three gates, not one big bang
The reason most programs of this kind fail: they are funded as one large initiative with a delivery date 18 months out. By month six, requirements have shifted, the vendor selection looks wrong, and the political will to change course is gone because too much has been committed. Gates force honest evaluation before commitment deepens.
Change management · starts day one, runs concurrent throughout
Executives — protect budget + time
The sponsor who must become a champion

Small Frame the burning platform — not “cool technology” but “cost of waiting another year.” What does the organization lose if this decision is deferred 12 months?

Medium Gate discipline: working software at each review, not slides. Protect the team from scope expansion requests before Gate 1 is cleared.

Large Shift from sponsor to champion. When they mention the program unprompted — in conversations you’re not in — the threshold has been crossed. That moment cannot be manufactured; it can only be earned.

Domain operators — the people whose work changes
Find the willing. Make them unfalsifiable.

Small Find the person who hates their current tool and has credibility. Mid-level, technically curious, slightly frustrated, respected for their workarounds. Not the loudest critic — the quiet builder.

Medium Pilot cohort embedded in live work — not a lab. Their feedback shapes the artifact re-imagination in real time. They become advocates before the program asks them to be.

Large Pilot becomes peer pressure. A room of skeptics watching a colleague do in 10 minutes what used to take 3 days. That moment is more persuasive than any ROI analysis — and it cannot be faked.

Legacy IT + data — the team whose stack changes
Co-build or they will resist. Give them the keys.

Small Map who owns what. The person who built the load-bearing Excel — the one that became the de facto ERP — is the person who must be made an architect, not a casualty.

Medium Co-build posture: their data gets a better home, not their systems get replaced. Frame it that way and frame it early. Give them authorship of the new stack before they have reason to defend the old one.

Large IT owns the COE. Give them the keys before it feels completely safe. That’s how it sticks permanently — internal ownership, not external dependency.

Gate 1 · Proceed / pivot / stop
Evidence: working prototype + pilot cohort reaction — not a deck
Activation layer · sequential after capability audit; concurrent once prototype is live
Prototype sprint — working artifact, not a document
Sequential: cannot start until capability audit is complete

Cannot begin without knowing what the data reality actually is. The capability audit is the mandatory prerequisite. Once it completes, the forward-deployed engineer and outcome engineer build a functional mock against real data in days — not a wireframe, not a demo, actual working software.

The prototype becomes the RFP. You ask vendors “can you match or extend this?” — not “can you interpret this 40-page functional spec?” The translation chain that exists because language is lossy gets replaced by code, which is not.

Outcome engineering — map data to decisions, not reports
Concurrent with artifact re-imagination once prototype is live
  • Translate each business outcome into specific system behaviors
  • Define what “correct” looks like for each decision type the system will touch
  • Set the bar for what constitutes a signal vs. noise for each persona
  • Validate continuously against pilot cohort reactions, not against a requirements document
Artifact re-imagination — from audit-ready to agent-ready
Concurrent with outcome engineering; feeds the data layer

Every artifact in scope gets re-examined: policies, taxonomies, KPIs, reporting templates, contracts, org charts. Each one is re-authored for agent operationalizability. This is the work that has no existing craft tradition and cannot be outsourced to a vendor — it requires the knowledge architect working alongside the domain operator pilot cohort.

Trust architecture — make the black box legible
Concurrent with workflow activation; neither ships without the other

Agentic systems only get adopted when operators can see why a recommendation was made. Decision lineage must be human-readable, not just machine-auditable. This is the trust and operations lead’s primary deliverable during the activation phase — and it cannot be bolted on after the fact.

Gate 2 · Expand / contract
Evidence: adoption rate + decision accuracy across pilot cohort
Technical infrastructure · capability audit first; all else concurrent once ontology is grounded
Capability audit — must happen first
Everything in the program gates on this. No exceptions.

Connect to existing systems. Extract signal about where operational friction lives, where decisions stall, where reconciliation fails. Produce a capability gap map grounded in actual data flows — not stated preferences.

This becomes the anchor for every vendor conversation and the foundation for the ontology. It is also the input that makes the prototype sprint possible. Nothing meaningful happens before this.

Ontology + knowledge graph — entities, edges, meaning
Sequential after audit; integration hub runs concurrent

Taxonomies are trees. Agents need graphs. Building the ontology forces the organization to answer questions it has never had to make explicit. These governance gaps surface here — and must be resolved, not deferred, because the agent-ready data layer cannot be built until the ontology establishes what entities and relationships exist.

Integration hub — connect what exists, don’t replace it
Concurrent with ontology build

Yardi, MRI, Excel, SharePoint, email — the sources of operational truth. The integration hub ingests, normalizes, and routes. The ontology determines what fields mean; the hub determines where they come from. These two workstreams run in parallel and converge at the agent-ready data layer.

Agent-ready data layer — structured for inference
Sequential after ontology is grounded; concurrent with front end

Audit-ready ≠ agent-ready. Data lineage ≠ decision lineage. The data layer must be structured for inference and grounded for decisions: context-scoped, temporally aware, and traceable to the human judgment it encodes. Cannot be built until the ontology establishes what entities and relationships exist and the integration hub surfaces where the data lives.

Small
Capability audit + stakeholder mapping + narrative build + prototype sprint. Days to weeks. People-cost dominated. No vendor selection yet.
Medium
Ontology v1 + integration hub + artifact re-imagination + pilot cohort + skills bridge. Weeks to a few months. Mix of people and infrastructure cost.
Large
Agent-ready data layer + scaled adoption + COE stood up + outcome engineers embedded in OpCos. Several months onward. Mix shifts toward OpEx. ROI measurable.
